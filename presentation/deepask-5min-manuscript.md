# DeepAsk Live - 5 minute manuscript

Use this with `deepask-5min-slides.html`.

## 0:00-0:30 - Slide 1: The survey that asks back

Hi. This is DeepAsk Live.

The shortest version is: it is a survey that asks back.

Most surveys ask one question, collect an answer, and then move on. DeepAsk does something slightly different. It lets a person answer in their own words, asks one neutral follow-up question, then turns the answer into a summary the participant can confirm.

For this demo, I am using ice cream as the wrapper because it is easy to understand and a little more fun than another serious dashboard.

## 0:30-1:10 - Slide 2: Most surveys taste flat

The problem is that many surveys taste flat.

They collect ratings, checkboxes, or a block of free text. But the interesting part is often hidden in the follow-up. Why did someone say that? What barrier are they pointing to? What change would actually help?

DeepAsk tries to capture that missing layer without turning the process into a long interview.

The participant still sees something simple. But behind it, the answer becomes more useful.

## 1:10-1:55 - Slide 3: One question, then one better question

Here is the participant flow.

The person answers one question. Then DeepAsk generates a neutral follow-up. The AI is not allowed to persuade, debate, judge, or push a political direction. It can only clarify.

So if someone says, "The event should be more inclusive," the useful question is not "Do you agree with this policy?" It is more like: "What specific change would make it feel more inclusive?"

In the ice cream version, the same mechanism becomes easy to see. Someone says what makes good ice cream good. The system asks for the missing detail.

## 1:55-2:45 - Slide 4: Original voice stays separate

This is the important governance part.

DeepAsk does not just overwrite the person with an AI summary.

The original text is kept separately from the AI interpretation. The participant sees the generated summary and can confirm or edit it. That means the machine is helping structure meaning, but the person still has the last word on what they meant.

For an organiser or researcher, this creates an audit trail. You can inspect the original answer, the follow-up, the participant's follow-up answer, the AI summary, and the final confirmed version.

That is the difference between "AI analysed the answers" and "we have a traceable workpack for each answer."

## 2:45-3:40 - Slide 5: From answers to group signals

After responses are submitted, DeepAsk has a second layer called Mind Hive.

Mind Hive is not a page where one person's answer gets published. It is a group layer. It looks for collective statements: agreements, tensions, minority concerns, and next questions.

Participants react to those group statements, not to each other.

That matters because the goal is not to rank people or expose individuals. The goal is to help the organiser see what the group is starting to say.

In a civic context, that can turn scattered answers into a better next conversation.

## 3:40-4:35 - Slide 6: A small tool, not a magic box

On the organiser side, DeepAsk stays practical.

There is a survey builder, a results view, a Mind Hive view, and an audit area. The organiser can export JSON or CSV when the data needs to move out of the demo.

The point is not to pretend this is a giant platform already.

The point is that the core loop works:

ask, clarify, summarise, confirm, aggregate, audit.

That is enough to show the shape of a better civic listening tool.

## 4:35-5:00 - Slide 7: Better listening, one scoop deeper

So the closing line is:

DeepAsk is better listening, one scoop deeper.

It does not replace human judgement. It does not decide what the group means. It helps people say what they mean, keeps the original voice separate, and gives organisers a clearer group signal to work from.

That is the whole tool: a small, auditable, adaptive survey system with a fun wrapper and a serious method underneath.
